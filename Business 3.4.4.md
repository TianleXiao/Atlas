## Current Liabilities
Current liabilities are the short-term debts of a business, which need to be repaid within twelve months. Typical examples include:
- Bank overdrafts allow customers to temporarily take out more money than is available in their bank account. This banking service enables pre-approved customers to borrow money and typically repay it within a few weeks/months in order to avoid high interest charges.
- Trade creditors - Suppliers may give trade credit (typically for 30 to 60 days), which needs to be repaid by the business at a future date.
- Other short-term loans - These are advances (loans) from a financier such as a commercial bank, that needs to be repaid within 12 months of the balance sheet date.
## Working Capital & Non-Current Liabilities
- **Working Capital** is the money your business needs to be able to operate from day to day.
$$Working \ Capital = Current \ assets - Current \ liabilities$$
- Many firms aim to have current assets which are twice the level of current liabilities, though businesses in some industries operate efficiently with lower ratios. (Chap 3.5.)
- **Non-current liabilities**: are the long-term debts of a business, falling due after 12 months of the balance sheet date. This refers to the long-term borrowings of the business, such as long-term loans, debentures and mortgages.
