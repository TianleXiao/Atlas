# Cash vs cash flow

Cash is a business's most liquid asset – it is notes and coins held in hand/in a safe as well as funds held in the business's bank accounts.
Cash flow refers to the movement of a business organization's cash inflows (cash received from the sale of goods and/or services) and cash outflows (used to pay for the costs of running the business).
Cash flow forecasting and cash flow statements are often used to measure the financial health of a business. This is because the comparison of cash inflows and cash outflows enables managers to determine whether the organization is able to pay its costs in order to maintain its business operations.
### Cash flow statement
A financial statement that aggregates a company's cash inflows and outflows from operations, investing, and financing over a set period of time.

## Profit vs cash flow
Cash flow is the movement of cash into and out of a business over a period of time.  
Profit is the value of sales revenue after all costs have been accounted for.  
Remember the good old formula: Profit = Sales revenue - Total costs  
- Businesses may experience negative cash flow even when they earn a profit:  
  - When a profitable business tries to expand too quickly, it runs out of cash.  
  - Although profit is earned when a sale is made, customers do not necessarily pay by cash as some opt for trade credit instead.  
  - Seasonal variations in demand (peak periods) can mean that there are certain times in the year when the firm might experience short-term liquidity problems.  
- Similarly, businesses with a positive cash flow may still experience a financial loss:  
  - A large cash inflow will occur from internal or external sources of finance.  
  - A company may sell an asset or a portion of the company to survive in times of economic crisis, so it could suffer a net loss while recording positive cash flow.  
  - The initial hype for a new product may bring in plenty of cash, but unless the firm is able to control its costs (e.g. marketing costs), it will not be profitable.

## Tesla’s race towards profitability

**Example: Tesla**

For example, both Amazon and Netflix took about 7 years of operation before becoming profitable. Tesla took just a little longer and did not reach break-even until 2020.

→ It is therefore more important to pay attention to the firm’s cash flow than to profit in the early stages. Ultimately, a business must have sufficient cash flow to continue operating, whether it is profitable or not.
