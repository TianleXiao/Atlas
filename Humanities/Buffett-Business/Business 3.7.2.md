## Short vs long working capital cycle
For some businesses, the working capital cycle is very short (quick) as they receive cash immediately from their sales.
- For example, cash/immediate payments are commonly used in hair salons, restaurants, cinemas, convenience stores, and taxis.
For other businesses, the working capital cycle is much longer (slow) due to the long production process and/or the high price of their products.
- For example, customers are likely to pay in instalments and/or purchase using credit terms for expensive products such as Lamborghini super sports cars, aged diamond rings.
They are also likely to wait longer for air Airbus aircraft to be delivered, due to the high demand and the more complicated, longer manufacturing process.

## Liquidity/cash flow position
The liquidity/cash flow position of an organization indicates the extent to which it has sufficient liquidity to continue its business activities.
The liquidity/cash position of a business is important as it shows its ability to repay short-term liabilities without having to rely on external sources of finance, which could dilute ownership and control and/or incur debt interest payments.  
The main method of measuring a firm's liquidity/cash position is liquidity ratio analysis.
Liquidity ratios examine an organization's ability to pay its short-term liabilities and debts, namely:
- **Current ratio**  
- **Acid test ratio** (also known as the quick ratio).
→ A liquidity problem (or cash flow problem) occurs when there is a lack of cash in the organization because its cash inflows are less than its cash outflows, i.e., it experiences negative net cash flow.

## Cash flow forecasts

Cash flow forecasting is a quantitative technique used by business managers to predict how cash is likely to flow into and out of the organization for a period of time, such as for the next twelve months, mainly to avoid future liquidity problems, or to support applications for bank loans.  
Net cash flow is the difference between inflows and outflows of cash over a period of time. The formula to calculate it is:

$$
Net \ Cash \ Flow = \text{Cash inflows} - \text{Cash outflows}
$$

- A positive net cash flow exists if the total cash inflows are greater than the total cash outflows for a given period of time, such as a month or per quarter.  
- A negative net cash flow/liquidity problem exists if the total outflows exceed the total inflows for a particular time period.  

→ Knowing in advance when the business is likely to face a period of cash shortages (or a liquidity problem) can help it to plan accordingly so that it can continue to operate.
